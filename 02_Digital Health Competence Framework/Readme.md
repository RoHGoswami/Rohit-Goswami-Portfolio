# A Novel Digital Health Competence Framework for Patients: Model Validation and Application in Italy

## Project Overview
This project is a Master's Thesis in Management Engineering developed at Politecnico di Milano. The primary objective was to design, statistically validate, and apply a novel Digital Health Competence Framework tailored specifically to the Italian population. The study aimed to uncover latent behavioral constructs, identify sociodemographic factors influencing digital health competence, and provide actionable, data-driven policy recommendations.

## The Research Objective
While advancements in digital healthcare have the potential to vastly improve service quality, an uneven distribution of digital health competence risks widening existing social and health inequalities. Vulnerable groups such as older individuals, those with lower education levels, and residents of rural areas often face significant disadvantages when trying to adopt digital health interventions. Existing frameworks often rely solely on psychometric assessments and require modernization to accurately reflect evolving technologies and to effectively assist in current policy design.

## Methodology
The research utilized a robust mixed-methods statistical approach on a dataset gathered via a custom questionnaire developed in collaboration with the Digital Health Observatory of Politecnico di Milano. The survey was administered to a random sample of 1,000 Italians via telephonic interviews.

* **Construct Validation (PLS-SEM):** Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modelling (with 5,000 bootstrap resamples) was used to validate the framework and establish structural relationships between four latent behaviors: Digital Literacy (DLIT), Health Literacy (HLIT), Digital Soft Skills (DSS), and eHealth Skills (EHS).
* **Sociodemographic Analysis:** A Multiple Linear Regression model incorporating a restricted cubic spline was employed to assess the impact of various sociodemographic factors. The cubic spline was specifically used to model factors like age, which typically exhibit non-linear trends.

## Key Findings

### Framework Validation & Behavioral Links
* The PLS-SEM confirmed the framework's robust statistical validity, demonstrating high internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha between 0.871 and 0.931) and strong convergent validity.
* **Digital Literacy (DLIT)** acts as a strong foundational predictor for **Digital Soft Skills (DSS)**, accounting for 82.4% of its variance.
* **Health Literacy (HLIT)** is a strong foundational predictor for **eHealth Skills (EHS)**, accounting for 87.4% of its variance.

### Sociodemographic Disparities
* **Age:** Exhibits a distinct non-linear relationship. Competence rises in early adulthood, plateaus, and sharply declines after age 50.
* **Education & Occupation:** Individuals with a lower secondary education or below, as well as specific occupational groups (such as housewives and lower-skilled general workers), show significantly diminished literacy scores.
* **Geography:** Substantial regional variations exist. Regions such as Lazio, Puglia, Basilicata, and Calabria scored notably higher than the baseline, suggesting the potential influence of localized infrastructure or regional initiatives.
* **Health Status:** Interestingly, having a pre-existing health condition positively correlates with higher digital and health literacy, likely due to increased necessity and exposure to health systems.

## Policy Recommendations
* **Shift to Bottom-Up Strategies:** Policymakers should transition from broad, top-down national directives to localized, bottom-up interventions focusing on specific regions, communities, local health authorities, or occupational groups.
* **Targeted Support:** Move away from generalized digitalization strategies. Instead, provide prioritized, tailored support for high-risk demographics identified in the study, specifically older adults, housewives, and those with lower educational backgrounds.
* **Incentive Alignment:** For digital health interventions to be sustainable, strategies must consider the perceived benefits, costs, and workload implications for all stakeholders across the healthcare value chain.
